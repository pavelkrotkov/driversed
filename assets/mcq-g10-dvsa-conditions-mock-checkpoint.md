# MCQ Bank: G10 DVSA Conditions + Mock Checkpoint

Units: 31-32
Use: End-of-group checkpoint after DVSA adverse-conditions practice and mock tests.

Pass target: 80% or better. Review every miss by error category.

## Q1. Heavy rain

You are watching a hazard-perception clip in heavy rain. The road surface is wet, spray is coming from vehicles ahead, and a pedestrian is near the curb. What should change in your prediction?

A. Focus only on the pedestrian because rain does not affect hazard timing
B. Expect longer stopping distance, reduced visibility, and earlier hazard development
C. Click later because hazards are harder to see in rain
D. Maintain normal following distance unless traffic stops

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Rain reduces grip and visibility, so hazards need earlier speed, space, and scanning adjustments.
**Error category:** Wet braking distance

## Q2. Low sun and glare

A low winter sun is shining toward you. You can see traffic ahead, but glare makes the far crosswalk and side streets hard to read. What is the best response?

A. Keep normal speed because the main lane is visible
B. Scan only the lane markings because pedestrians will be obvious
C. Look away from the road until the glare passes
D. Reduce speed, increase following distance, and treat unclear edges as possible hazard zones

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Glare lowers contrast and hides road users at edges, so you need more time and space before a hazard becomes obvious.
**Error category:** Glare and poor contrast

## Q3. Night edge scanning

You are driving at night on a road with parked cars and side streets. Headlights show only part of the scene, and the sidewalk edge is dark. What should you be most alert for?

A. Only hazards directly inside your headlight beam
B. Hidden pedestrians, cyclists, or vehicles emerging from low-visibility areas
C. Speed changes only when another driver flashes headlights
D. Road signs, because road users are easier to see at night

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Night driving narrows useful vision. Hazards can develop from dark edges before they are fully visible.
**Error category:** Night scanning

## Q4. Snow near a bend

A clip shows snow piled along the curb, tire tracks in the lane, and a vehicle ahead approaching a bend. What is the safest prediction?

A. Snow matters only if it is actively falling
B. The lane is safe because tire tracks prove grip is normal
C. The vehicle ahead may brake or steer earlier, and your own stopping distance would be longer
D. Hazards are easier to predict because everyone drives slowly

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Snow can reduce lane width, hide edges, and increase stopping distance even when traffic is moving.
**Error category:** Snow space management

## Q5. Shaded ice

The road looks mostly clear, but shaded patches remain near an intersection after freezing weather. A car ahead begins slowing before the junction. What should you assume?

A. The driver is being overcautious, so prepare to pass
B. Ice only matters if you can see white snow on the road
C. Shaded areas may be icy, so expect reduced grip and earlier braking
D. The safest response is to brake sharply at the last second

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Ice can be hard to see, especially in shade. Junctions also collect braking and turning conflicts, so speed and space should adjust early.
**Error category:** Ice recognition

## Q6. Poor contrast miss

During a DVSA adverse-conditions practice session, you miss a hazard because a dark-clothed pedestrian blends into a rainy background. How should you review the miss?

A. Classify it as poor contrast, then look for earlier context cues such as crossings, bus stops, and road edges
B. Mark it as bad luck and move on
C. Memorize the exact second when the pedestrian appeared
D. Decide the clip was unfair because the pedestrian was hard to see

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Reviewing by error type helps transfer learning to new clips. Poor contrast should make you scan likely emergence zones earlier.
**Error category:** Mock review by error type

## Q7. Score chasing

In a mock test, you clicked every possible hazard very early and scored poorly on several clips. What is the best adjustment?

A. Click only after the hazard is directly in your lane
B. Stop clicking uncertain hazards entirely
C. Click repeatedly whenever visibility is poor
D. Identify potential hazards early, but click when one starts developing and affects the driver

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Hazard perception is not about clicking everything. The score depends on recognizing when a potential hazard becomes developing.
**Error category:** Score chasing

## Q8. Inconsistent mock scores

Across several mock tests, your scores vary widely. You do well in daylight clips but miss hazards in rain, night, and glare. What is the best next step?

A. Review misses by condition and build a consistent routine: speed, space, scan, then click developing hazards
B. Keep taking random mock tests until one score is high enough
C. Practice only the clips you already score well on
D. Click earlier in every clip regardless of what changes

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Consistency comes from a repeatable method, not from optimizing for one passing score. Conditions should trigger specific speed, space, and scan changes.
**Error category:** Consistency and condition transfer
