# MCQ Bank: G8 DVSA Format Transfer + First Paid Calibration

Units: 26-28
Use: End-of-group checkpoint after DVSA free clips, DVSA explainer, and first paid scored session.

Pass target: 80% or better. Review every miss by error category.

## Q1. Potential pedestrian

You are watching a DVSA-style clip. A pedestrian is standing on the sidewalk near a parked car. They have not moved toward the road yet. What should you do?

A. Click immediately because any pedestrian is a developing hazard
B. Keep watching the pedestrian and nearby hidden space, but wait for movement that affects your driving
C. Ignore the pedestrian unless they enter your lane
D. Click repeatedly until the pedestrian moves

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A potential hazard becomes a developing hazard when it changes so that you may need to slow, change position, or stop.
**Error category:** Potential-vs-developing timing

## Q2. Cyclist moving around a parked vehicle

A cyclist ahead is riding steadily near the curb. A few seconds later, they look over their shoulder and begin moving outward around a parked vehicle. When is the best DVSA-style click?

A. When the cyclist first appears in the scene
B. When the cyclist is still riding steadily at the curb
C. When the cyclist starts moving outward and may affect your path
D. Only after you would need emergency braking

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The developing hazard starts when the cyclist's action begins to create a possible conflict with your driving path.
**Error category:** Developing-hazard recognition

## Q3. Too-early click

You see a parked van near a crosswalk. It blocks your view, but no person or vehicle has emerged yet. You click at the first sight of the van and receive no score for the hazard. What most likely happened?

A. The click was too early because the hazard had not started developing
B. The van was never relevant to hazard perception
C. DVSA clips only score vehicle hazards
D. You should have clicked continuously from the start

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Blocked sightlines are important to monitor, but scoring usually begins when a hazard develops, not merely when a possible hazard exists.
**Error category:** Early click

## Q4. Too-late click

In a clip, a child steps from behind a parked car toward the road. You notice but wait until the child is directly in front of your vehicle before clicking. What is the main problem?

A. You clicked too early
B. You clicked too late and likely missed the higher scoring part of the window
C. You should not click for pedestrians
D. You should click only after the vehicle has stopped

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** DVSA scoring rewards recognizing the developing hazard early enough to respond smoothly. Waiting until the conflict is obvious reduces or loses credit.
**Error category:** Late click

## Q5. Pattern clicking in DVSA format

During a scored DVSA session, you click every two seconds throughout the clip to avoid missing the hazard. What is the risk of this strategy?

A. It guarantees a high score because one click will land in the window
B. It may be treated as pattern clicking and does not show real hazard judgment
C. It is required because each clip has many scored hazards
D. It is safer than watching for changes in the scene

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Clicks should be tied to observed hazard development. Regular pattern clicking can be penalized and weakens the training value.
**Error category:** Pattern clicking

## Q6. First paid session

You are starting your first paid DVSA online scored session after using the free clips and explainer. What is the best goal for this first session?

A. Treat it as calibration: use your normal strategy and record where your misses happen
B. Click as often as possible to learn the scoring system
C. Pause after every possible hazard and look up the answer
D. Focus only on getting a perfect score before moving on

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** The first scored session is most useful as a baseline. It should reveal whether errors are early clicks, late clicks, missed occlusions, or overclicking.
**Error category:** Calibration mindset

## Q7. Reviewing weak clips

After the first scored session, you review 6 clips where your score was weak. What is the most useful review method?

A. Memorize the exact second of each correct click
B. Sort each miss by category, such as early click, late click, missed hidden-space cue, or pattern clicking
C. Rewatch only the highest scoring clips
D. Assume every miss came from the same cause

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Reviewing a small set by error category turns the scored session into targeted practice instead of score chasing.
**Error category:** Review by category

## Q8. Distractors vs scored hazard

A clip contains several potential hazards: parked cars, a side road, and pedestrians on the sidewalk. Only one hazard later develops when a vehicle begins emerging from the side road. What should your clicking reflect?

A. Click once for every object that could possibly become a hazard
B. Click only when the side-road vehicle begins to create a real driving conflict
C. Avoid clicking because there were too many possible hazards
D. Click at the start of the clip before anything changes

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** DVSA format requires attention to many potential hazards, but scoring depends on identifying the one that actually develops.
**Error category:** Distractor vs scored hazard
